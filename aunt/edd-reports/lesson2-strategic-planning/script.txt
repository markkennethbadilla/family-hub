SLIDE 1 — TITLE
Good morning, everyone. My report covers Topic 4.1 of Strategic Planning and Management — Defining Organizational Purpose through mission statement development. In short, this is about how an organization says clearly, in one or two sentences, why it exists.


SLIDE 2 — WHERE MY TOPIC SITS
Before we begin, let me place my topic. Chapter 4 has three parts: 4.1 the mission, 4.2 strategic goals, and 4.3 key performance indicators. My report focuses only on the first — 4.1 — because it is the foundation the other two build on.


SLIDE 3 — WHAT THIS REPORT WILL COVER
Here is our roadmap. First, what a mission statement is, and what it is not. Second, why defining purpose matters before anything else. Third, how to develop, draft, and refine one. And finally, some examples, common mistakes, and a simple checklist.


SLIDE 4 — WHAT A MISSION STATEMENT IS
So, what is a mission statement? It is a concise declaration of an organization's core purpose and reason for existing — what it does, for whom, and to what end. In plain terms: one or two clear sentences that say why we exist.


SLIDE 5 — WHAT A MISSION STATEMENT IS NOT
It also helps to say what it is not. It is not a slogan — not a catchy advertising line. It is not a list of goals — those come later, in 4.2. And it is not the vision — the vision is the future dream, while the mission is the present, everyday work.


SLIDE 6 — MISSION IS NOT THE SAME AS VISION
Let me make that distinction clear. The vision is the dream — where we hope to be someday; it faces the future. The mission is the present — what we do every day, and why. My topic is the mission: the work of today, not the dream of tomorrow.


SLIDE 7 — THREE QUESTIONS A MISSION ANSWERS
A strong mission answers three simple questions. WHO do we serve? WHAT do we do for them? And WHY does it matter? If our statement answers all three, in plain language, then the mission is sound.


SLIDE 8 — THE THREE PARTS, UP CLOSE
Let us look at those three parts more closely. The who is the people we exist to serve — our students, families, the community. The what is the core service or change we provide to them. And the why is the deeper value or purpose behind the work.


SLIDE 9 — WHAT MAKES A MISSION STRONG
A good mission statement has four qualities. It is clear — plain words anyone can understand. It is concise — short enough to remember. It is purpose-driven — it names the real reason we exist. And it is distinctive — it fits this organization, not just any organization.


SLIDE 10 — WHY DEFINING PURPOSE COMES FIRST
Why does this come first? Four reasons. Alignment — it keeps everyone pulling in the same direction. It is a decision filter — guiding choices when priorities compete. It gives identity — it tells people what we stand for. And it is the foundation — the goals and the measures that come later are all built on top of it.


SLIDE 11 — DEVELOPING A MISSION STATEMENT
Now to the heart of my topic: how we actually develop one.


SLIDE 12 — HOW TO DEVELOP ONE, STEP BY STEP
There are four steps. Step one, identify who you serve — the people who benefit. Step two, state what you do for them — your core service. Step three, explain why it matters — the deeper purpose. And step four, draft it, then trim — write it out, then cut until only the essential remains.


SLIDE 13 — BUILDING ONE, PART BY PART
Here it is in action. Who: every child. What: a safe place to learn skills and values. Why: to be ready for life. Put together, it becomes — "To give every child a safe place to learn the skills and values they need for life." Short, clear, memorable, and it answers all three questions.


SLIDE 14 — THE PATTERN ACROSS DIFFERENT SETTINGS
The same pattern works anywhere. A school might say, "To help every learner gain the skills and confidence for life." A clinic, "To give our community affordable, caring health services." A library, "To open the world of reading and ideas to everyone we serve." Different settings, same simple structure.


SLIDE 15 — WEAK MISSION VS STRONG MISSION
Let me show the difference. A weak mission says, "To be the best school in the region." It sounds nice, but it says nothing about who we serve or why. A strong mission says, "To help every learner in our town gain the skills and confidence for life." Now the who, the what, and the why are all clear, and people can act on it.


SLIDE 16 — COMMON MISTAKES TO AVOID
There are common mistakes to avoid. Too long — a paragraph no one can remember. Too vague — words like "excellence" that could mean anything. All about us — forgetting the people we serve. And copy-pasted — a generic line that could fit any organization.


SLIDE 17 — REFINING THE DRAFT
Refining matters. Here is a wordy draft: "Our mission is to provide a high-quality, holistic, learner-centered educational experience that nurtures excellence." Refined, it becomes: "To help every learner grow in skill, character, and confidence." We cut the jargon and kept the meaning. Shorter is stronger.


SLIDE 18 — THE MISSION ANCHORS EVERYTHING
One more crucial idea: alignment. The mission sits at the center. Every daily choice, every goal, and every priority should trace back to it. When they do, the organization moves as one. When they do not, energy leaks away.


SLIDE 19 — KEY TAKEAWAYS
To summarize. A mission says, in plain words, why we exist. A strong one answers who, what, and why. We develop it by naming the parts, drafting, then trimming. And it comes first, because everything else in strategy is built on it.

That concludes my report on Defining Organizational Purpose — purpose, made clear. Thank you, and I welcome your questions.
