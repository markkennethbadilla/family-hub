SLIDE 1 — TITLE
Good morning, everyone. My report covers Topic 4.1 of Strategic Planning and Management — Defining Organizational Purpose through mission statement development. In short, this is about how an organization says clearly, in one or two sentences, why it exists.


SLIDE 2 — WHERE MY TOPIC SITS
Before we begin, let me place my topic. Chapter 4 has three parts: 4.1 the mission, 4.2 strategic goals, and 4.3 key performance indicators. My report focuses only on the first — 4.1 — because it is the foundation that the other two are built on. Get the purpose right, and everything that follows has direction.


SLIDE 3 — WHAT A MISSION STATEMENT IS
So, what is a mission statement? It is a concise declaration of an organization's core purpose and reason for existing — what it does, for whom, and to what end. In plain terms: one or two clear sentences that say why we exist. It is the anchor for everything else.


SLIDE 4 — MISSION IS NOT THE SAME AS VISION
A quick but important distinction. The vision is the dream — where we hope to be someday; it faces the future. The mission is the present — what we do every day, and why. They are easy to confuse, but my topic is the mission: the work of today, not the dream of tomorrow.


SLIDE 5 — THREE QUESTIONS A MISSION ANSWERS
A strong mission answers three simple questions. WHO do we serve? WHAT do we do for them? And WHY does it matter? If our statement answers all three, in plain language, then the mission is sound.


SLIDE 6 — WHAT MAKES A MISSION STRONG
A good mission statement has four qualities. It is clear — plain words anyone can understand. It is concise — short enough to remember. It is purpose-driven — it names the real reason we exist. And it is distinctive — it fits this organization, not just any organization.


SLIDE 7 — HOW TO DEVELOP A MISSION STATEMENT
This is the heart of my topic: how we actually develop one. Step one, identify who you serve — the people who benefit. Step two, state what you do for them — your core service. Step three, explain why it matters — the deeper purpose. And step four, draft it, then trim — write it out, then cut until only the essential remains.


SLIDE 8 — BUILDING ONE, STEP BY STEP
Here it is in action. Who: every child. What: a safe place to learn skills and values. Why: to be ready for life. Put together, it becomes — "To give every child a safe place to learn the skills and values they need for life." Short, clear, memorable, and it answers all three questions.


SLIDE 9 — WEAK MISSION VS STRONG MISSION
Let me show the difference. A weak mission says, "To be the best school in the region." It sounds nice, but it says nothing about who we serve or why. A strong mission says, "To help every learner in our town gain the skills and confidence for life." Now the who, the what, and the why are all clear, and people can actually act on it.


SLIDE 10 — WHY DEFINING PURPOSE COMES FIRST
Why does this come first? Four reasons. Alignment — it keeps everyone pulling in the same direction. It is a decision filter — guiding choices when priorities compete. It gives identity — it tells people what we stand for. And it is the foundation — the goals and the measures that come later are all built on top of it.


SLIDE 11 — KEY TAKEAWAYS
To summarize. A mission says, in plain words, why we exist. A strong one answers who, what, and why. We develop it by naming the parts, drafting, then trimming. And it comes first, because everything else in strategy is built on it.
