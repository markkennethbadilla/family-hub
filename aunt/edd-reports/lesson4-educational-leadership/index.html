703 · 11.2The construct

Defining the constructWhat ethical principles are

Ethical principlesThe agreed standards of right conduct that guide how a leader makes decisions and treats people — especially when the choice is difficult.
In plain terms: the rules of "doing right" that a school administrator lives by.

703 · 11.2Rationale

Why principles, not just rules

Rules cannot cover every situation. Principles give a leader a steady way to decide what is right even when no rule fits the case in front of them.

The Core Ethical Principles

703 · 11.2Principle 1

Principle 1Integrity & honesty

Be truthful and consistent; keep your word. What you say and what you do should match.
703 · 11.2Principle 2

Principle 2Fairness & justice

Treat everyone equally; decide without favoritism, and give each person what is due to them.
703 · 11.2Principle 3

Principle 3Respect for persons

Honor the dignity of every person — each student and staff member — in how you speak and decide.
703 · 11.2Principle 4

Principle 4Accountability

Own your decisions and answer for their results — do not pass the blame when things go wrong.
703 · 11.2Principle 5

Principle 5Confidentiality

Protect private information about students and staff; share it only when it is right and necessary.
703 · 11.2Principle 6

Principle 6Service & the common good

Put the welfare of learners and the community above personal interest. The role is a trust, not a privilege.

703 · 11.2In practice

A principle in action

A hard choiceA relative applies for a teaching post and is not the most qualified.

The principles of fairness and integrity say: choose the best candidate, openly and on merit — even when it is personally uncomfortable.
703 · 11.2In practice

A second hard choice

Confidentiality vs. pressureA staff member shares something private in confidence. Others press you to repeat it.

Confidentiality and respect say: protect it — share only what is truly necessary, and only with those who must know.
703 · 11.2Judgment

When principles seem to collide

Sometimes two principles pull in different directions. The leader weighs them honestly, chooses the one that best serves people and the common good, and can explain the reason openly.
703 · 11.2Significance

Why these principles matter

  • TrustPeople trust a leader whose decisions are fair and honest.
  • ConsistencyPrinciples keep decisions steady from one case to the next.
  • Good exampleA school learns ethics from how its leaders behave.
703 · 11.2Key takeaways

Key takeaways

What
Ethical principles are the standards of right conduct.
The core
Integrity, fairness, respect, accountability.
Also
Confidentiality and service to the common good.
Why
They build trust, consistency, and a good example.
