Topic 11.2: Principles of Ethics in Educational Administration
Reporter: Rosemarie Mayet Rizon


HOW TO USE THIS SCRIPT
Each part matches a slide. The ideas are at a doctoral level, but every term is explained in plain, everyday language. Speak naturally; you do not need to read it word for word.


SLIDE 1 — TITLE
Good morning, everyone. My report covers Topic 11.2 of Educational Leadership — the Principles of Ethics in Educational Administration. In short, this is about the core principles that guide fair and honest decisions in running a school.


SLIDE 2 — WHERE MY TOPIC SITS
Let me first place my topic. Chapter 11 has several parts — values and ethics, principles of ethics, values-based leadership, and good governance. My report focuses only on 11.2 — the principles that turn our values into ethical practice.


SLIDE 3 — WHAT THIS REPORT WILL COVER
Here is our roadmap. First, what ethical principles are. Second, why we need principles, not just rules. Third, the core principles themselves, one by one. And finally, how they work in practice, and why they matter.


SLIDE 4 — WHAT ETHICAL PRINCIPLES ARE
So what are ethical principles? They are the agreed standards of right conduct that guide how a leader makes decisions and treats people — especially when the choice is difficult. In plain terms: they are the rules of doing right that a school administrator lives by.


SLIDE 5 — FROM VALUES TO PRINCIPLES TO PRACTICE
It helps to see how this fits together. First come our values — what we believe. Those values become principles — how we decide; that is our topic, 11.2. And principles become practice — what we actually do. Principles are the bridge between belief and action.


SLIDE 6 — WHY PRINCIPLES, NOT JUST RULES
Why do we need principles, and not just rules? Because rules cannot cover every situation. Principles give a leader a steady way to decide what is right, even when no specific rule fits the case in front of them.


SLIDE 7 — THE CORE ETHICAL PRINCIPLES
Now to the heart of my topic — the core principles themselves. There are six, and I will take them one at a time.


SLIDE 8 — PRINCIPLE 1: INTEGRITY AND HONESTY
The first is integrity and honesty. Be truthful and consistent; keep your word. What you say and what you do should match. A leader without integrity cannot be trusted, no matter how skilled.


SLIDE 9 — PRINCIPLE 2: FAIRNESS AND JUSTICE
The second is fairness and justice. Treat everyone equally; decide without favoritism, and give each person what is due to them. Fairness is what makes people accept even decisions they dislike.


SLIDE 10 — PRINCIPLE 3: RESPECT FOR PERSONS
The third is respect for persons. Honor the dignity of every person — each student and each staff member — in how you speak and how you decide. Respect costs nothing and earns everything.


SLIDE 11 — PRINCIPLE 4: ACCOUNTABILITY
The fourth is accountability. Own your decisions and answer for their results. Do not pass the blame when things go wrong. A leader who is accountable models it for the whole school.


SLIDE 12 — PRINCIPLE 5: CONFIDENTIALITY
The fifth is confidentiality. Protect private information about students and staff; share it only when it is right and necessary. People confide in leaders they can trust to be discreet.


SLIDE 13 — PRINCIPLE 6: SERVICE AND THE COMMON GOOD
The sixth is service and the common good. Put the welfare of learners and the community above personal interest. The role is a trust to be used for others, not a privilege to be enjoyed.


SLIDE 14 — THE PRINCIPLES AT A GLANCE
So, all six together: integrity, fairness, respect, accountability, confidentiality, and service to the common good. These are the standards a school leader carries into every decision.


SLIDE 15 — A PRINCIPLE IN ACTION
Let me give an example of a hard choice. Suppose a relative applies for a teaching post, but is not the most qualified candidate. The principles of fairness and integrity tell us what to do: choose the best candidate, openly and on merit — even when that is personally uncomfortable.


SLIDE 16 — A SECOND HARD CHOICE
Here is a second case. A staff member shares something private in confidence, and others press you to repeat it. Confidentiality and respect tell us what to do: protect it — share only what is truly necessary, and only with those who must know.


SLIDE 17 — WHEN PRINCIPLES SEEM TO COLLIDE
Sometimes two principles seem to pull in different directions. When that happens, the leader weighs them honestly, chooses the one that best serves people and the common good, and is able to explain the reason openly. Good ethics can stand the light of day.


SLIDE 18 — WHY THESE PRINCIPLES MATTER
Why do these principles matter? Trust — people trust a leader whose decisions are fair and honest. Consistency — principles keep our decisions steady from one case to the next. And good example — a school learns its ethics from how its leaders behave.


SLIDE 19 — KEY TAKEAWAYS
To summarize. Ethical principles are the standards of right conduct. The core ones are integrity, fairness, respect, and accountability. We also hold confidentiality and service to the common good. And they matter because they build trust, consistency, and a good example.


SLIDE 20 — THANK YOU
That concludes my report on the Principles of Ethics in Educational Administration — leading by principle. Thank you, and I welcome your questions.
