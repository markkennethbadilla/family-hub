708 · 5.1The construct

Defining the constructWhat contextualization means

ContextualizationDesigning and adapting learning resources so they connect to the learner's real environment — their place, culture, language, and daily experience.
In plain terms: teaching with examples learners already recognize from their own lives.
708 · 5.1Rationale

Why context makes learning stick

When new ideas are tied to something a learner already knows, they are easier to understand, easier to remember, and easier to use in real life.
708 · 5.1Principles

The heart of the topicThe principles of contextualization

  • RelevanceUse examples from the learner's own community and surroundings.
  • Prior knowledgeBuild new lessons on what learners already understand.
  • AuthenticityDraw on real situations, not invented or distant ones.
  • Meaning over memorizationAim for understanding learners can apply, not rote recall.

708 · 5.1Practice

Applying the principle

  1. Know your learnersTheir place, language, work, and everyday experiences.
  2. Find local examplesSwap distant references for familiar, real ones.
  3. Adapt, don't diluteKeep the standard; change only the wrapping.
708 · 5.1Significance

Why contextualization matters

  • EngagementLearners pay attention to what feels relevant to them.
  • ComprehensionFamiliar context lowers the barrier to new ideas.
  • EquityIt meets learners where they are, whatever their background.
708 · 5.1Key takeaways

Key takeaways

Meaning

Principles
Relevance, prior knowledge, authenticity, meaning.

Adapt the example; keep the standard.
