SLIDE 3 — WHAT AN EXPERIMENTAL DESIGN IS
So what is an experimental design? It is a research method that tests cause and effect by changing one thing on purpose and comparing the results against a group that was left unchanged. In plain terms: we try the program on one group, not on another, and see what differs.


SLIDE 4 — THE RANDOMIZED CONTROLLED TRIAL
The strongest form is the randomized controlled trial, or RCT. We take our participants and split them at random into two groups. One — the treatment group — receives the program. The other — the control group — does not. Then we compare their outcomes. Because they started out alike, any difference at the end points to the program.


SLIDE 5 — THREE BUILDING BLOCKS
There are three building blocks. Randomized — people are placed into groups by chance, which removes hidden bias. A control group — a comparison group that does not receive the program. And pre and post measures — we measure before and after, so the change can be seen clearly.


SLIDE 6 — TWO KINDS OF VALIDITY
We also judge a study by its validity, and there are two kinds. Internal validity asks: are we sure the program caused the change, and not something else — is the result real and not a fluke? External validity asks: would the result hold true elsewhere, with other people or places — does it generalize beyond this one study?


SLIDE 7 — WHY THIS DESIGN IS THE STRONGEST
Why is this the strongest design? Because it isolates the cause — the control group rules out other explanations. It removes bias — random assignment keeps the groups fair and comparable. And it produces trusted evidence — its results carry the most weight in evaluation.


SLIDE 8 — KEY TAKEAWAYS
To summarize. The purpose is to prove a program truly causes a result. The RCT does this by randomizing, treating one group, and comparing to a control. Validity comes in two forms — internal means real, external means generalizable. And this design is the most trusted way to show cause.
