714 · 5.1The problem

The question it answers

Did our program cause the improvement — or would it have happened anyway? Experimental design is how we tell the difference.
714 · 5.1The construct

Defining the constructWhat an experimental design is

Experimental designA research method that tests cause and effect by changing one thing on purpose and comparing the results against a group that was left unchanged.
In plain terms: try the program on one group, not on another, and see what differs.

714 · 5.1Building block 1

Building block 1Why assign at random?

Random assignment spreads every hidden difference — age, ability, background — evenly across both groups, so the only real difference left is the program itself.
714 · 5.1Building block 2

Building block 2The control group

Control groupA comparison group that does not receive the program, kept alongside the treatment group.
It shows what would have happened without the program — the "what if we did nothing" picture.

714 · 5.1Worked example

A school example

Testing a reading programTake 100 pupils. Randomly split them: 50 join the new reading program, 50 keep the usual lessons. Test everyone before and after. If the program group improves more, the program likely caused it.

Judging the study

Internal & External Validity

714 · 5.1Validity

Internal validityIs the result real?

Are we sure the program caused the change, and not something else? Strong internal validity means the result is real, not a fluke.
714 · 5.1Validity

External validityDoes it hold elsewhere?

Would the result hold true elsewhere, with other people or in other places? Strong external validity means it generalizes beyond this one study.
714 · 5.1Watch out

Things that can weaken validity

  • DropoutsPeople leaving the study can unbalance the groups.
  • Too small a sampleA few people can't represent everyone.
  • SpilloverThe control group accidentally gets some of the program.
714 · 5.1Significance

Why this design is the strongest

  • Isolates the causeThe control group rules out other explanations.
  • Removes biasRandom assignment keeps the groups fair.
  • Trusted evidenceIts results carry the most weight in evaluation.
714 · 5.1Honest limits

When it is hard to use

Practical & ethical limitsSometimes we cannot randomly withhold a program — it may be unfair to deny help, or simply impractical. In those cases researchers turn to other designs (5.2 onward).
714 · 5.1In practice

When to choose an experimental design

  • You need proof of causeNot just that two things move together.
  • You can assign at randomFairly and ethically.
  • The stakes are highA big decision rests on the evidence.
714 · 5.1Key takeaways

Key takeaways
